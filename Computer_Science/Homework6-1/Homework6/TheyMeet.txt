Always willing to give a hand
I met a girl once, with long brown hair, and the deepest of deep green eyes. Through that which she lacked, she gained so much more. I was eleven./n

I went to Sunday-School so God could teach me the lessons of life. Maybe it worked. What I learned I will never forget. The girl sat down beside me /n
in a cold steel chair in the cold brick room, on a cold Sunday morning./n

I stared at the gas heater on the other side of the room. Others prayed for those less fortunate, but I prayed for warmth. I gave up and turned towards/n

her - she smiled without hesitation or malcontent./n

Beneath the din of others conferring, we laughed, we joked, we shared stories. She had just been with her Dad on a canoeing adventure. My uncle had taught/n

me how to fish for trout from his yellow ski-boat./n

The warm discussion made the cold room bearable, and the time passed quickly. We were placed in activity groups based on our age. She was ten. We sat together/n

again. The move exposed us to the cold. Everyone sat rubbing their hands under the table./n

Cold, hard lumps of clay were placed before each of us as we were given a divine dut - to create from them God’s own creations. Moving her hands towards the /n

table, what I saw next shocked me at first. Each hand bore nothing more than a little finger, but one which I found could perform the task of five./n

With her clay, she fashioned a magnificent elephant, a remarkable giraffe, Both made my feeble mouse seem all the more insignificant./n

Over the years, our friendship grew stronger as we saw more of one another. I saw her conquer challenges; flying foxes to shoelaces, hammers to watch bands./n

Nothing stood in her way, she worked hard and was thankful for even the smallest of victories. She laughed when the Reverend gave her the well-meaning “always/n
